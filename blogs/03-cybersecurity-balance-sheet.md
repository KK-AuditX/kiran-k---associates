## Cybersecurity Risk on the Balance Sheet: How CFOs Should Quantify and Report Cyber Threats

Cyber risk is now a financial risk. CFOs who cannot translate a cyberattack into rupee impact are leaving their organisations exposed. Here's a practical framework to quantify, report, and manage cybersecurity risk.
For most of the last decade, cybersecurity was treated as an IT department problem. The CISO would present a risk register, the board would nod, and the conversation would move on to revenue targets.
That era is ending.
A single ransomware attack can shut down operations for days. A data breach can trigger regulatory penalties, customer attrition, and litigation simultaneously. In India, SEBI now requires board-level accountability for cyber risk at all regulated entities. The message from regulators, investors, and rating agencies is consistent: cybersecurity is a financial risk, and the CFO owns a share of it.

## Why CFOs Cannot Ignore This Anymore
Consider what happens financially when a serious cyber incident occurs:
•	Revenue stops if operations go offline
•	Emergency response costs — forensic investigators, legal counsel, PR, ransom negotiations
•	Regulatory fines under CERT-In, DPDP Act, RBI, or SEBI frameworks
•	Customer compensation and contractual penalties
•	Reputational damage leading to long-term revenue loss
•	Increased insurance premiums or loss of cyber insurance coverage
None of these appear on a traditional risk register in financial terms. That is the core problem. If a risk cannot be expressed in rupees, it will not receive adequate budget.

## A Framework for Quantifying Cyber Risk
The key is to move from qualitative risk language ("high likelihood, high impact") to financial estimates. Here is a practical approach:

## Step 1: Map Critical Systems to Revenue
Identify which systems, if compromised or shut down, directly impact revenue generation or cash flow. For example: a payment gateway going down for 24 hours has a calculable revenue cost.


## Step 2: Scenario-Based Loss Modelling
Run three scenarios — optimistic (minor incident, contained quickly), base case (moderate breach, 48-hour disruption), and worst case (ransomware, regulatory investigation, data exfiltration). Assign financial values to each — revenue loss, remediation cost, penalty estimates.

## Step 3: Calculate Annual Loss Expectancy (ALE)
A common formula used globally:
ALE = Probability of Incident × Financial Impact
For example, if there is a 20% probability of a meaningful cyber incident in a year, and the estimated financial impact is ₹2 crore, the Annual Loss Expectancy is ₹40 lakhs. That number can directly inform your cybersecurity budget.

## Step 4: Establish a Cyber Incident Reserve
Leading CFOs are now setting aside a board-approved contingency reserve — typically 1–2% of annual operating expenditure — specifically for cyber incident response. This covers forensic investigation costs, legal fees, and short-term operational recovery without disrupting the main budget mid-crisis.

## Step 5: Integrate Cyber Risk into Board Reporting
SEBI's Cybersecurity and Cyber Resilience Framework (CSCRF), updated in August 2025, requires SEBI-regulated entities to report cyber risk at the board level with clear financial context. Even for non-regulated entities, embedding a "cyber risk" line into the quarterly board pack — with scenario-based estimates and mitigation status — is rapidly becoming a governance best practice.

## Cyber Insurance: A CFO's Emerging Tool

Cyber insurance in India is still maturing, but it is growing rapidly. A well-structured policy can cover breach response costs, regulatory fines (where insurable), business interruption losses, and third-party liability. However, insurers are increasingly requiring evidence of security controls — including IS Audit reports — before issuing or renewing policies. The audit and the insurance strategy are therefore closely linked.

## What CFOs Should Do This Quarter
•	Sit down with your IT/CISO team and translate the top 3 cyber risks into rupee estimates
•	Review whether your current cybersecurity budget aligns with your quantified risk exposure
•	Commission an IS Audit to independently validate your security posture
•	Ensure cyber risk is a standing agenda item at board and audit committee meetings
Cybersecurity is not just a technical matter. It is a financial one. CFOs who own this conversation — rather than defer it — will be significantly better positioned to protect their organisations.

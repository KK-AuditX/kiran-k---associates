## GST Compliance for SaaS and Digital Businesses: What You're Getting Wrong
SaaS companies, digital platforms, and online service providers face unique and often misunderstood GST obligations in India. From OIDAR classification to reverse charge, here's what you need to get right.
Running a SaaS business or a digital platform in India looks straightforward on the surface — you build a product, users pay you, and growth follows. The GST implications, however, are anything but straightforward, and the mistakes made by digital businesses in this area are consistently significant.
Here is a plain-language breakdown of the most important GST obligations for Indian SaaS and digital businesses — and the errors that trigger notices and penalties.

## Understanding OIDAR: The Most Critical Classification
OIDAR stands for Online Information and Database Access or Retrieval Services. Under Indian GST law, if your service is delivered over the internet, is highly automated, and requires minimal human intervention, it likely falls under this category.
Examples of OIDAR services:
•	Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) products
•	Cloud computing services
•	Online gaming and entertainment platforms
•	Digital content (e-books, music, video streaming)
•	Online advertising services
•	Website hosting and domain services
Why it matters: OIDAR services are taxed at 18% GST (IGST) in India. If you are an Indian SaaS company supplying to Indian customers, this applies to you. If you are a foreign SaaS provider with Indian users, it applies to you too — and since October 1, 2023, the earlier exemption for foreign OIDAR providers supplying to individuals and government entities has been removed entirely.

## Common Mistakes Indian SaaS Companies Make

## Mistake 1: Incorrectly classifying B2B vs B2C transactions
For B2B transactions (your client is a GST-registered business), the reverse charge mechanism applies for services received from overseas providers. The Indian recipient pays GST, not the foreign provider.
For B2C transactions (your client is an individual or unregistered entity), the supplier — even if based outside India — must register for GST in India and collect and remit the tax directly.
Many businesses blur this line and end up with incorrect GST treatment across their entire client base.

## Mistake 2: Not registering under GST at all
Some early-stage SaaS founders believe the GST registration threshold (₹20 lakhs for most states) means they are exempt. However, OIDAR service providers are exempt from this threshold — if you are providing OIDAR services to customers in India, you are required to register regardless of your turnover.

## Mistake 3: Wrong place of supply determination
For digital services, the place of supply rules are different from physical goods. The place of supply is the location of the recipient, not the supplier. This determines whether CGST+SGST or IGST applies, and getting this wrong affects your entire return filing.

## Mistake 4: Incorrect treatment of cross-border SaaS subscriptions
If your company subscribes to international SaaS tools — Salesforce, HubSpot, AWS, Zoom — you are required to pay GST under the reverse charge mechanism and report it in your returns. Many businesses simply pay the foreign invoice and ignore the GST implication entirely, creating a compliance gap that surfaces during scrutiny.

## Mistake 5: Missing the GSTR-5A filing requirement
Foreign OIDAR providers serving Indian B2C customers must file GSTR-5A by the 20th of every month. Indian OIDAR providers follow the standard GSTR-1/3B cycle. Mixing these up — or missing the GSTR-5A deadline — results in late fees and potential deregistration risk.

## Input Tax Credit (ITC): What You Can and Cannot Claim
SaaS businesses can claim ITC on GST paid for inputs used in the course of business — server costs, software subscriptions, professional services, and office expenses. However, ITC is blocked on certain items (motor vehicles for personal use, food and beverages, etc.). Additionally, ITC is only available if your supplier has correctly filed their returns — a fact that an IS Audit or compliance review can verify through GST portal reconciliations.

## What the GST Portal Can Now Flag
The GST Network (GSTN) has significantly improved its data analytics capabilities. It cross-matches:
•	Your GSTR-1 (outward supplies declared) with your clients' GSTR-2B (input tax credit claimed)
•	Your e-invoicing data with your return filings
•	Turnover declared in GST returns with income tax returns
Discrepancies generate automated notices. For SaaS businesses with large transaction volumes and complex client types, systematic reconciliation — not just last-minute filing — is essential.

## Practical Steps to Get This Right
11.	Correctly classify all your services as OIDAR or non-OIDAR
12.	Segregate your customer base into GST-registered and unregistered categories
13.	Apply the correct place of supply rules for each transaction type
14.	Ensure reverse charge liabilities on foreign SaaS subscriptions are paid and reported
15.	Reconcile GSTR-2B with your purchase register monthly, not quarterly
16.	Engage a CA familiar with both technology business models and GST to review your compliance posture at least annually
GST compliance for digital businesses is not just a tax matter — it is directly linked to your financial audit trail, investor due diligence readiness, and regulatory standing. Getting it wrong is expensive. Getting it right from the start costs far less.

## The DPDP Act 2023 and Its Impact on Information System Audits in India
India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 is now in force. It brings mandatory audits, data protection officers, and penalties up to ₹250 crore. Here's what businesses need to understand — and do.
India finally has a comprehensive data protection law. The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (DPDP Act) — notified on 11 August 2023 — fundamentally changes the way Indian businesses must handle personal data.
For companies that process personal data — which, let's be honest, is practically every business today — this law is not just an HR policy matter or a legal checkbox. It has direct and significant implications for Information System Audits.

## What the DPDP Act Requires
The Act classifies entities that process personal data as Data Fiduciaries. The most heavily regulated among these are Significant Data Fiduciaries (SDFs) — organisations that process large volumes of sensitive personal data and are formally notified by the Central Government.
For SDFs, the Act mandates:
•	Appointing a Data Protection Officer (DPO) based in India, accountable to the Board of Directors
•	Conducting Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIA) before undertaking high-risk data processing activities
•	Undergoing periodic independent audits of their data processing systems and safeguards
•	Reporting data breaches to the Data Protection Board of India and affected individuals without delay
Penalties for non-compliance are substantial — up to ₹250 crore for violations involving SDFs, and up to ₹150 crore for failure to implement appropriate security safeguards.

## Where IS Audit Comes In
This is where Information System Audits become directly relevant. The DPDP Act requires businesses to demonstrate that their data handling systems are technically robust, not just legally compliant on paper.
An IS Auditor reviews:
•	Data mapping and inventory — What personal data does the organisation collect? Where is it stored? Who has access?
•	Consent management systems — Are consent records properly maintained and auditable?
•	Access controls — Are data access rights restricted to those with a legitimate need?
•	Encryption and security safeguards — Is personal data protected in transit and at rest?
•	Breach detection systems — Can the organisation detect a data breach in time to meet reporting requirements?
•	Cross-border transfer compliance — If data is being sent outside India, are the conditions under the Act satisfied?
These are not theoretical questions. They require evidence — log files, access records, system configurations, audit trails. An IS Audit is the mechanism through which this evidence is collected, tested, and reported.

## Aligning DPDP Compliance with Other Regulations
One of the practical challenges Indian businesses face is that the DPDP Act does not exist in isolation. Regulated entities must simultaneously comply with:
•	RBI's cybersecurity framework (for banks, NBFCs, fintechs)
•	SEBI's CSCRF (for capital market intermediaries)
•	CERT-In Directions 2022 (applicable to all organisations)
•	IRDAI guidelines (for insurance companies)
An integrated IS Audit approach — one that maps DPDP requirements against these sectoral obligations — avoids duplication, reduces cost, and ensures nothing falls through the cracks.

## What Businesses Should Do Now
Regardless of whether your organisation is formally designated an SDF, the DPDP Act sets expectations for all data fiduciaries. Waiting for mandatory notification is a risk. Practical steps to take now:
6.	Conduct a data audit — understand exactly what personal data you hold
7.	Review your consent mechanisms — are they active, specific, and documented?
8.	Assess your security safeguards against DPDP requirements
9.	Engage an IS Auditor to evaluate technical readiness and identify gaps
10.	Build a breach response plan — the Act's notification timelines are strict
The DPDP Act represents India's most significant step toward digital accountability. For businesses that embrace it proactively, it is an opportunity to build genuine trust with customers and regulators alike.
